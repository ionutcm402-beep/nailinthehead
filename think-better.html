The Story

You think better than you think.

Most people don't have a thinking problem. They have a thinking-alone problem. When you have to explain something out loud, you understand it better. When someone asks you 'why?', you find the reasons. When a challenge is raised, you find the weaknesses before they become mistakes.

The problem is that access to this kind of rigorous, honest thinking partnership has always been limited to people who could afford coaches, mentors, or therapists — or who happened to know the right people.

AI is the first time in history that this kind of thinking partnership is available to everyone. Not instead of human support — alongside it. Available at 2am. Available when the decision can't wait. Available with no judgement and no agenda.

How It Works

The path
forward.

You don't need to understand AI to use it well. You just need to know what to ask.

01
Name the real decision
Most people struggle to think clearly because they're thinking about the wrong thing. Before anything else, ask AI to help you identify what you're actually deciding — not the symptoms, but the actual question.
02
Argue the other side
The fastest way to know if your thinking is sound is to have it challenged. Ask AI to argue against your position as strongly as possible. If it can't, you're on solid ground. If it can, you needed to know.
03
Find the assumptions
Every decision rests on assumptions. Most of them are never examined. Ask AI: 'What am I assuming to be true in order for this reasoning to hold?' Then question each one.
04
Think in longer time frames
Most regret comes from thinking too short-term. Ask AI: 'How will I feel about this decision in 5 years? What would my 70-year-old self think?' Perspective is the most powerful thinking tool there is.
05
Write it down and read it back
The act of explaining your thinking in writing is itself clarifying. Use AI as the audience. Tell it your thinking. Ask it to summarise what it heard. The gap between what you meant and what it understood is where clarity lives.

Prompt · The Decision Room
Challenge my thinking on this decision
For every important decision you are circling. The one you keep coming back to without resolution.
I am trying to decide whether to [describe the decision]. I am currently leaning towards [your current inclination] because [your reasons]. Before I decide, I want you to: challenge my reasoning, identify what I might be missing, ask me the questions I should be asking myself, and tell me what a wise, experienced person who cares about my long-term wellbeing would say.
✓ Found the one thing I'd been avoiding thinking about. Made my decision the same evening.
Prompt · Steel Man
Help me think through both sides honestly
For when you know what you want to do but suspect you might be fooling yourself.
I am considering [decision or position]. I believe I should [what you want to do]. Please give me: the strongest possible argument FOR this choice (steel-manning my position), the strongest possible argument AGAINST it, the three most important questions I should answer before deciding, and a recommendation from someone who has no stake in my decision.
✓ Realised I was rationalising, not reasoning. Changed my decision. Have not regretted it.
Prompt · Find My Assumptions
Where might my thinking be wrong?
For when you have a belief or a plan and you want to stress-test it before acting on it.
Here is my current thinking on [situation or plan]: [explain it]. I want you to: identify the key assumptions I am making that I might not be aware of, challenge the weakest parts of my reasoning, tell me what information I would need to be more confident I am right, and identify the most likely way this thinking could lead me badly wrong.
✓ Found a gap in my plan that would have been very expensive to discover later.
Prompt · The Clarity Room
I am confused — help me understand what I actually think
For when your own thinking is unclear, contradictory, or just stuck.
I am feeling confused or uncertain about [topic or situation]. I'm not sure what I think. Please ask me questions — one at a time — to help me work out what I actually believe and why. Don't tell me what to think. Help me find it through conversation.
✓ Spent an hour in conversation. By the end I knew exactly what I believed. I just needed help finding it.
Prompt · Long View
What would my future self think of this?
For when you are too close to a decision and need perspective.
I am facing this situation: [describe it]. I am currently thinking of [what you are considering doing]. Please give me: what my 70-year-old self would most likely think about this choice, what I might regret not doing or not saying, what the person I want to become would choose, and what is actually at stake here that I might be underestimating.
✓ I read the response and understood that I was about to make the safe choice, not the right one.
Prompt · Explain It Back
Help me understand my own thinking
Sometimes you don't know what you think until you try to explain it. This makes that conversation happen.
I want to think through [topic, problem, or situation]. I am going to explain my current thinking to you — as fully as I can — and then I want you to: summarise back to me what you heard, identify what seems unclear or contradictory, and ask me one question that you think would help me understand this better.
✓ The gap between what I meant and what it heard showed me exactly where my thinking was fuzzy.
Common Questions

Everything
you wondered.

Is this the same as therapy?+
No. AI is a thinking tool, not a therapeutic relationship. It can help you think more clearly, challenge your assumptions, and see situations from different angles. For emotional support, grief, trauma, or mental health — please seek a human professional. These two things complement rather than replace each other.
What if I don't agree with what AI says?+
Good. Disagreement means you're thinking. Push back. Ask it to justify its position. Ask it to consider your counter-argument. The point is not to be told what to think — it's to think more rigorously than you can alone.
Can this help with decisions at work?+
Yes. This is one of its most powerful applications. Strategic decisions, difficult conversations, team problems, career choices — all of these benefit enormously from rigorous external challenge to your thinking.
What about really personal decisions — relationships, family?+
AI can be genuinely useful here for thinking through complex emotional situations clearly. It won't tell you what to do. But it can help you understand what you actually want, what you're afraid of, and what your options really are.
I tend to overthink. Won't this make it worse?+
The opposite, in our experience. Overthinking often happens because you're running the same thoughts in circles without resolution. Externalising the conversation — writing it down, having it with AI — breaks the loop. Most overthinkers find that one good AI conversation cuts through weeks of circular thinking.
